ESTONIA — TURKEY: THE DUAL-HUB LANE.

Published June 2026 Reading time ~6 min Corridor EE — TR

Estonia–Turkey is a quiet lane with a loud advantage. Most forwarders cover either the EU end or the Turkish end and subcontract the other half. CANXANSA is an Estonian-registered company with direct operations in Istanbul — both ends of this lane under one roof. This guide covers the three ways cargo moves between Tallinn and Istanbul, what the dual-hub setup changes in practice, and the paperwork involved.

/ 01Three ways to move it

ModeTransit timeRouting
Air2–4 daysTallinn (TLL) → Istanbul (IST/SAW), from 45 kg
Road (FTL/LTL)7–10 daysTallinn → Poland → Balkans → Turkey, door-to-door
Sea (FCL/LCL)14–20 daysMuuga → Mersin/Istanbul/Izmir, via Baltic–Med liner services

Quick mode logic: air for electronics, pharma and high-value goods where days matter; road for medium-volume cargo wanting door-to-door simplicity with GPS tracking; sea for larger volumes where cost per unit wins. Most sea shipments tranship via Hamburg or Antwerp; LCL adds 2–3 days for consolidation.

/ 02What dual-hub actually changes

  • No agent margins. One contract, one invoice, one team responsible end-to-end — instead of two forwarders quoting margin on each other.
  • EU customs in-house. As an Estonian (EU) entity we file EU import/export documentation directly, handle EORI, and offer VAT-compliant invoicing — including import VAT deferment where Article 71 of the VAT Directive applies.
  • Turkish side under direct control. Customs clearance (gümrük), Istanbul/Mersin/Izmir port operations and IST/SAW airport handling are run by our own Istanbul presence, not a partner agent.
  • Onward reach. From the Turkey hub, cargo continues to the Middle East and Asia — Tallinn becomes your gateway beyond the EU.

/ 03Customs and documents

The lane benefits from the EU–Turkey Customs Union: industrial goods in free circulation move duty-free with an A.TR movement certificate (agricultural products use EUR.1 instead). A standard shipment carries:

  • Commercial invoice and packing list
  • EX-A export declaration (EU side)
  • A.TR (industrial) or EUR.1 (agricultural)
  • CMR (road), bill of lading (sea) or airway bill (air)
  • Turkish customs declaration (gümrük beyannamesi) at import

For the full document logic on EU–Turkey lanes, see our Turkey–Germany road freight guide — the customs mechanics are the same family.

/ 04Who uses this lane

Typical flows: Estonian and Baltic electronics, machinery and timber products heading to Turkish manufacturers and distributors; Turkish textiles, automotive parts and food products heading north. Because volumes are thinner than on the German lane, forwarder choice matters more — schedules are not daily, and a forwarder who controls both ends can hold cargo to the right departure instead of the next available agent slot.

/ 05Frequently asked questions

Why is the road option slower than on the Germany lane?
Distance. Tallinn–Istanbul by truck crosses the full length of Eastern Europe — Poland, Slovakia, Hungary and the Balkans — before reaching Turkey, hence 7–10 days against 3–6 on Turkey–Germany. It is still the best door-to-door option for medium volumes on this lane.
Can Baltic neighbours use the same setup?
Yes. The Muuga consolidation point and road routings serve Latvia and Lithuania naturally, and our EU customs authority covers any member state. The dual-hub advantage applies to the whole Baltic–Turkey flow.
What does VAT deferment mean in practice?
Where Article 71 arrangements apply, import VAT is accounted for in the VAT return rather than paid in cash at the border — a real cash-flow difference for importers. Whether your flow qualifies depends on the setup; we review it as part of the quote.
