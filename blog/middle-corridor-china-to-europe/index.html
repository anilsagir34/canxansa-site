THE MIDDLE CORRIDOR: CHINA → EUROPE.

Published June 2026 Reading time ~6 min Corridor CN — TR — EU

The fastest growing trade route between Asia and Europe doesn't go through Russia. The Middle Corridor — the Trans-Caspian route from China through Kazakhstan, across the Caspian Sea, through Azerbaijan and into Turkey — has moved from geopolitical footnote to mainstream logistics option. This guide explains how it works, what it costs relative to sea and air, and when routing your China–Europe cargo through a Turkey hub makes commercial sense.

/ 01What the Middle Corridor actually is

Rail departs from Chinese inland manufacturing hubs — Xi'an, Chengdu, Chongqing — and runs west through Kazakhstan to the Caspian shore. Containers cross the Caspian by ferry, continue through Azerbaijan into Turkey, and from the Turkey hub move onward to European destinations by rail or road.

The route exists as an alternative to the Northern Corridor (through Russia) and to all-water routings. Since the Russia–Ukraine disruptions, it has become Asia's main politically stable land gateway to Europe — and Turkey sits at its western end, which is precisely why a Turkey-hub forwarder is well positioned on this lane.

The positioning is simple: rail saves roughly 10–15 days against sea freight and is typically 30–50% cheaper than air. Per cubic metre it costs more than ocean freight — you are paying for those saved days.

/ 03When rail through Turkey makes sense

  • Cargo too urgent for the water, too heavy for the air. Seasonal goods, replenishment stock running late, project cargo with a fixed installation date.
  • Inland China origins. If your supplier sits in Xi'an, Chengdu or Chongqing, rail departs from the doorstep — no pre-carriage to a coastal port.
  • Predictability over headline speed. Avoiding congested transhipment ports can matter more than the fastest quoted day count.
Honest caveat

Transit times on this corridor depend on border crossings and seasonal Caspian ferry conditions. A serious forwarder quotes a range and explains the current state of the route — not a single optimistic number.

/ 04The multimodal play: sea to Turkey, road onward

A frequently overlooked combination: ocean freight from China to a Turkish gateway (Mersin, Istanbul or Izmir), then road freight onward into Europe. You capture most of the ocean-freight economics while shaving the slowest final leg, and the cargo enters the EU by truck on flexible schedules. For shippers with European customers across several countries, the Turkey hub also works as a distribution point — consolidate at the hub, then dispatch per order.

/ 05Customs in transit

Goods routed through Turkey under transit procedures (T1 / TIR) are not subject to Turkish import duties — the cargo passes through bonded. Transit documentation, customs bonds and onward EU entry are handled by the forwarder. Where cargo is cleared in Turkey before final EU delivery, preferential arrangements under the EU–Turkey Customs Union may apply to eligible goods.

/ 06Frequently asked questions

Is the Middle Corridor reliable enough for regular shipments?
Yes, with the right expectations. Capacity and infrastructure have grown quickly, but transit times still vary with border and ferry conditions. For regular flows we recommend planning against the conservative end of the 18–25 day range and letting the upside surprise you.
Can small shipments use the corridor, or is it FCL only?
Rail on this corridor is primarily FCL (20' and 40' containers). For smaller consignments, the multimodal option — LCL sea freight to Turkey, then groupage road onward — is usually the practical route. Air via Istanbul covers urgent small cargo from 45 kg.
Which European destinations work best via the Turkey hub?
Central and Western European hubs — Germany, the Netherlands, Italy, Spain — are well served by onward road and intra-EU connections from Turkey. The further north the final destination, the more the routing decision depends on the specific lane; we quote per case.
